> Ooo... The dark side is strong in this one. -laugh-

Eh, I'm not sure I fall into either category. I'm not just a hacker, but
I'm also not a "programmer" in title, though I find that does make up
a fair amount of my daily activity at work. I'm a Senior Network Engineer;
unfortunately this title is quite ambiguous; for me it means I'm 
responsible for all the Routers, Switches, WAN links, DNS, DHCP, Firewalls,
Internet etc. for a relatively large network. I'm not responsible (thank
deity of choice) for anything dealing with the user authentication, email
infrastructure (exchange)... nearly anything that I might have to deal
with a traditional OS on; the exceptions being DNS, DHCP, and some
firewalls.  The good thing is that it's my choice what OS those services
run on.  (Well, I am part of a 6 person team, so its not just me that
makes those decisions.)

So, it is in this capacity that I look around and go "Ooo, I could write
a script that would make this tedious task less tedious!" Then go about
writing it. So, my stuff is just a bunch of tools, once I get them to
work, I leave 'em alone generally. Thus, for me its not as important to
ensure that the things are written as cleanly as possible. And really,
once the thing works, I tend to just think of it as another command
available under *NIX, and start forgetting to tack on the '.pl' part.
So, although 'f' did start out as 'f.pl', it was through this process
that I dropped the '.pl'.

I have perl/expect routines to change passwords and community strings
on nearly all our heterogeneous network equipment, routines to backup
the configurations of these boxes, routines to watch for latency 
problems, routines to comb through syslog information, plus lots of
little tiny scripts that make my life just a bit easier.

Some of the larger scripts would probably benefit from use of the -w
or use strict, but then I'd have to spend the time to clean them up
rather than working on the next cool tool :)
